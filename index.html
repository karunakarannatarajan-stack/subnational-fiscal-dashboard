FRBM & 15th Finance Commission Compliance Matrix

Central Govt Norms

The FRBM Act (2003) and 15th Finance Commission (2021–26) set binding fiscal limits for states. Fiscal deficit is capped at 3% of GSDP (base), revenue deficit must be zero or surplus, debt-to-GSDP must trend below 32.5%, and interest payments should stay under 10% of revenue receipts. An additional 0.5% of GSDP in borrowing is allowed for power sector reform compliance.

Cross-State Revenue Quality & Fiscal Autonomy

A state's fiscal autonomy is determined by its **Own Tax Revenue (SOTR) to GSDP ratio** vs. its **Federal Transfers Share (devolutions + grants-in-aid) to total revenue receipts**.

Bottom-Right (High Autonomy): States here generate high own tax revenues and rely less on the Federal government. This grants them significant policy flexibility to design state-specific welfare and capital programs.

Top-Left (High Dependency): States here have a high dependency on Central Transfers. They are highly exposed to macroeconomic shocks, changes in Finance Commission devolution formulae, and Central policy shifts.

Central Devolution & Grants Comparison (% of Revenue Receipts) Central Devolution & Grants Comparison:
Constitutes the combined total of tax devolution (statutory share of central taxes recommended by the Finance Commission) and all central grants (both grants-in-aid and scheme-based transfers) expressed as a percentage of the state's total Revenue Receipts. It measures the state's overall reliance on federal fiscal transfers.

Federal Favoritism: Direct Central Investment & Projects (% of Revenue Receipts) Federal Favoritism (Direct Central Investment):
Constitutes direct central capital expenditures in the state that do not pass through the state budget (e.g., major projects by NHAI, Railways, or other Central Ministries) expressed as a percentage of the state's total Revenue Receipts. Higher ratios indicate a disproportionate concentration of federal development investment in specific states.

Grants-in-Aid (Article 275) (% of Revenue Receipts) Grants-in-Aid (Article 275):
Constitutes statutory non-shareable grants given under Article 275 of the Constitution, expressed as a percentage of the state's total Revenue Receipts. Components include Post-Devolution Revenue Deficit Grants (to cover gaps after tax devolution), grants for local bodies (panchayats and municipalities), and special allocations like disaster management funds.

Centrally Sponsored Schemes (CSS) & Central Sector Schemes (% of Revenue Receipts) Centrally Sponsored Schemes (CSS):
Constitutes direct project-based funding where the Centre co-finances schemes executed by states, expressed as a percentage of the state's total Revenue Receipts. Covers major social and infra schemes such as MGNREGA, PMGSY (rural roads), and Swachh Bharat.

Key Transfer Mechanism Definitions

1. Tax Devolution (Statutory Transfers): The constitutional division of net tax proceeds collected by the Union, distributed to states based on the formula recommended by the Finance Commission (e.g., share of population, forest cover, income distance, and fiscal performance). These represent untied funds that states can spend at their own discretion.

2. Grants-in-Aid (Article 275): Non-shareable statutory funds recommended by the Finance Commission and given to specific states to bridge systemic post-devolution deficits (Revenue Deficit Grants), alongside special performance-linked grants for local bodies (rural/urban) and disaster management funding.

3. Centrally Sponsored Schemes (CSS) & Central Sector Schemes: Scheme-specific financial assistance. CSS represents co-financed programs executed by states where the Centre provides a set share of capital (e.g., 60:40 or 90:10 for special category states) for national priority areas (MGNREGA, PMGSY). Central Sector Schemes are 100% funded directly by the Centre.

4. Direct Central Investment (Off-Budget Capital Outlay): Direct capital expenditure by Central Ministries and agencies (such as NHAI for highways or Indian Railways) inside the state. These funds do not flow through the state budget or treasury but represent direct federal asset creation within the state's territory.

Net Central Transfers (% of Revenue Receipts) Net Central Transfers:
Net federal resource flow as a percentage of Revenue Receipts. Calculated as Aggregate Central Transfers (Tax Devolution + Direct Central Investment + Grants-in-Aid + CSS) minus the GST revenue sent from the state to the Centre.

Positive values indicate the state receives more from the Centre than it sends as GST. Negative values indicate a net outflow — the state sends more to the Centre in GST than it receives back in all federal transfers combined.

Total Generated Revenues (Rupees Billion) Total Generated Revenues (Rupees Billion):
Measures the absolute total revenue receipts generated by the state's economic activity (State's Own Revenue Receipts + GST Sent to the Central Government) without including any federal transfers back to the state government. This reflects the true internal revenue-generating capacity of the state.

GST Transferred to Centre (% of All State-Generated Tax Revenue) GST Transferred to Centre (% of All State-Generated Tax Revenue):
Measures the GST (CGST + Central IGST) sent to the Central government as a proportion of all taxes extracted from the state's economy — including the state's own revenue receipts plus the full central tax pool drawn from the state (income tax, corporate tax, customs, excise duties, and GST).

Federal Budgetary Transfers Received vs Total Taxes Paid (%) Federal Budgetary Transfers Received vs Total Taxes Paid:
Measures what percentage of all taxes extracted from the state's economy (State Own Revenue + total central taxes including income tax, corporate tax, customs, excise, GST) is returned to the state government as constitutional budgetary transfers (Tax Devolution + Grants-in-Aid + CSS only, excluding off-budget direct central investment in railways and infrastructure).

FC Chairman Period Vertical Share Change Macro Context

Structural Shifts in Vertical Devolution

14th FC Paradigm Shift (2015–2020): Under Y.V. Reddy, the vertical share made a historic 10-percentage-point jump from 32% to 42% — the single largest increase in India's fiscal federalism history. The rationale was to move away from tied, scheme-based central grants (which carry conditionalities) toward unconditional, untied tax devolution, giving states greater fiscal autonomy.

15th FC Adjustment (2020–2026): Under N.K. Singh, the vertical share was marginally reduced from 42% to 41% to account for the creation of two new Union Territories — J&K and Ladakh — carved out of the erstwhile state of Jammu & Kashmir, which were removed from the divisible pool sharing formula since UTs are centrally funded.

12th FC Era (2005–2010): The first commission to operate fully under the post-80th Constitutional Amendment regime (2000), which pooled income tax and corporation tax into a single divisible pool alongside excise duties. The 30.5% share reflected this consolidated base for the first full five-year cycle.

16th FC Status (2026–2031): Under Arvind Panagariya, the commission is expected to maintain the 41% status quo to preserve macroeconomic stability. A higher devolution is debated since Central government's sovereign obligations (defence, debt service) place pressures to retain a larger share, while states argue for higher shares to fund SDG-linked expenditures.

Sovereign Tax Surrender: The SST-to-GST Trade-Off & States' Real Fiscal Share

Macroeconomic Audit

While the statutory vertical devolution rate rose from 32% to 42% (now 41%), this headline jump did not increase the states' relative share of combined national tax collections. When accounting for the sovereign tax powers surrendered by states under the 101st Constitutional Amendment (GST), the Centre's relative position has strengthened. Before 2017, states controlled 100% of their Sales Tax / VAT base. Today, they share that consumption tax base with the Centre, which retains a significant portion.

Combined Tax Revenue (CTR) Share Trajectory (2000–2031)

This chart tracks the states' final share of combined Centre + State taxes (including devolution) showing the stagnation over the last 15 years:

Key Takeaway: In relative terms, states' actual final share of India's combined taxes has remained flat at around 61% to 62%. The statutory jump from 32% to 42% was largely neutralized by the surrender of independent sales tax bases and the rise of central cesses.

The Mathematics of Tax Surrender (₹100 of GST Base)

Before GST, if a state collected ₹100 in State Sales Tax (SST), it kept all ₹100. Today, under the unified GST framework:

Total Consumer GST Paid ₹100.00
1. SGST (State GST) — Direct to State ₹50.00
2. CGST (Central GST) — Goes to Centre GTR ₹50.00
↳ Less Central Cesses & Surcharges (~21% of GTR) -₹10.50
↳ Shareable Divisible Pool Remaining ₹39.50
↳ State Devolution Share (41% of pool) +₹16.20
↳ Centre's Retained Pool Share (59% of pool) +₹23.30
Total Received by State (SGST + Devolution) ₹66.20 (66.2%)
Total Retained by Centre (Cesses + Pool Share) ₹33.80 (33.8%)

* States effectively surrendered 33.8% of a consumption tax base they once controlled entirely, to compensate for devolution from other central tax pools.

endent sales tax bases and the rise of non-shareable central cesses.

1. Erosion via Cesses

By raising tax rates via non-shareable cesses (e.g. road cess, oil surcharges), the Centre shrunk the divisible pool from 90% to 79% of Gross Tax Revenues, clawing back states' headline gains.

2. Loss of Rate Autonomy

Pre-GST, states could unilaterally raise Sales Tax / VAT rates on local commodities during fiscal stress. Today, they cannot change rates without consensus in the GST Council.

3. Expansion of Tied Grants

Central resources increasingly flow via Centrally Sponsored Schemes (CSS). These tied grants require states to co-finance (40% match) and follow strict spending guidelines, reducing flexible spending.
